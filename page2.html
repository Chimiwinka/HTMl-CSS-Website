Barbara Liskov (November 7th, 1939), is an American computer scientist. She currently teaches at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology. She was the first woman to ever have been granted a doctorate in computer science in the US. She is also a Turing Award[1] winner. That is quite the achievement!

She became interested in computers and programming when she moved to Boston and began working at Mitre Corporation. After a year there, Barbara took a programming job at Harvard where she worked on language translation. She enjoyed it so much that she decided to go back to school, and graduated from Stanford University with a P.h.D. The topic of her thesis was a computer program to play chess, and for that she developed the killer heuristics.

After that she returned to Mitre to work as a researcher. Then in 1971, shortly after her experiments with Venus and presenting her paper on the topic, she was invited for a position at MIT. Liskov left Mitre and joined the MIT faculty as a professor in Computer Science.

She worked on many major projects, including the design and implementation of CLU/Argus, the first high-level language to support implementation of distributed programs and to demonstrate the technique of promise pipelining.

Liskov's subsequent work has mainly been in the area of distributed systems, which use several computers connected by a network. Her research has covered many aspects of operating systems and computation, including important work on object-oriented database systems, garbage collection, caching, persistence, recovery, fault tolerance, security, decentralized information flow, modular upgrading of distributed systems, geographic routing, and practical Byzantine fault tolerance.

[1] The ACM A. M. Turing Award is an annual prize given by the Association for Computing Machinery (ACM) for contributions "of lasting and major technical importance to the computer field".
